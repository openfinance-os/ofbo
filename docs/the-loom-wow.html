The proof. We built the Open Finance Back Office on it — 135 shipped features across ~740 files (synthetic data, permanently non-production): an autonomous agent harness runs the full lifecycle — discover, define, develop, deliver — and stops for a human at exactly three decisions. The part the map misses. A harness alone is generic. It compounds into a moat a competitor cannot simply buy.

The guardrail. Agents do the building and the recurring assurance work; humans stay accountable and hold four-eyes approval (see AI governance, below).

The ask. Back the Loom as the regulated entity's default way to build — and take one or two real domains through it next.

The ideaWe built a way to build

What the Loom is

The Loom is our name for how a regulated entity builds software with AI — set it up once, then weave any solution on it. The weaving metaphor is exact, and it maps one-to-one onto the method:

Warp (threads under tension) = the always-on controls — four-eyes, audit, lineage, gates. · Harnesses = discovery (find the right problem) + delivery (build it under control). · Shuttle = the AI agents the harness pulls in to weave continuously. · Pattern = the institution's own context — its brain. · Cloth = shipped, audit-ready software.

Open Finance is the example. The Loom is the deliverable.

The moat test. If a competitor copied our entire codebase tomorrow, what would they still lack? Everything that makes it ours — the institution's accumulated, governed context. That is the brain (see The brain, below), and it cannot be copied — only built.

Most delivery programmes produce one thing: the thing. This one produced two — the Open Finance Back Office, and the Loom, the method that built it. The Loom is the asset that lasts. It takes an ambiguous idea, finds the problem worth solving, and turns it into live, audit-ready software with the controls built in — and it does so the same way every time.

Open Finance is simply the first domain we ran it through. Payments, lending, onboarding, servicing, risk — each can reuse the same loom instead of reinventing one. The point for leadership is not "we shipped a back office." It is "we now have a repeatable, controlled way to ship anything regulated."

The harnessesA double diamond — discover the right problem, then deliver it

At the heart of the Loom are two harnesses, arranged as a double diamond. The first diamond is about the problem; the second is about the solution. Each opens up to explore, then narrows to decide. They meet at one artifact — the agreed problem statement, or PRD — the hand-off from discovery to delivery.

Discovery — finding the right problem

Before a line of code, the discovery harness turns a challenge into evidence and an agreed problem. It diverges to gather evidence widely — users, market, regulation, data, current state — then converges on a framed problem, a business case, and a scope, with a funded go/no-go and an optional RFP when it is a build-vs-buy call. Crucially, a discovery is allowed to fail: stopping the wrong problem early is a win, not a waste.

Not a slide — built. The discovery harness now exists in the repo as a working mirror of delivery: a binding canon (discovery/DISCOVERY.md), a discovery skill and a brand-render skill, two reviewer agents (data-governance, discovery-boundary), and eight CI-enforced gates — D1 problem framing · D2 evidence · D3 scope & stakeholders · D4 no-solutioning · D5 synthesis integrity · D6 data-governance feasibility · D7 brand conformance · D8 tangibility (the live deployment runs a ninth, D9). It has already taken two real problems end-to-end (consent-lifecycle hygiene and fee-variance reconciliation), each emitting cited evidence, a framed problem, a business case and an on-brand prototype (real .docx/.pptx/.xlsx). It is solution-agnostic by construction: the domain mounts through two seams — a data-risk register and a brand profile — so the same machinery runs discovery for a different institution unchanged.

Delivery — shipping it under control

The delivery harness turns the PRD into working software on rails. Every change is specification-first, four-eyes approved, and auto-deployed to a live demo; nothing ships without clearing build, security, integration and data-lineage gates; and no real customer data ever touches the system. Compliance and auditability are properties of the line — not a scramble before an audit.

Three decisions a person still owns

The agent proposes; a human disposes. Everywhere else the machine is faster and more consistent — but these three carry regulatory accountability, so they never run on AI judgement alone.

◆ Waist gate — HG-0007

discovery hand-off

A human accepts the framed problem before any delivery story exists — the four-eyes of discovery; kill-criteria can stop here.

◆ Merge gate — HG-0001

four-eyes merge to main

A human approves & merges every PR on a protected branch — no self-merge of an agent’s own work.

◆ Prod gate — HG-0005 / Q5

production approval

A human approves the release to production — evidenced at release time with a sealed bundle.

The whole design. Everything between these points — discovery, evidence, design, code, tests, contract checks, lineage, the pull request itself — is produced and verified by the harness. The human reads, questions, and decides. Machines do the work; people own the calls that matter.

The context brainThree dimensions — regulated context, solution domain, how the bank works

A harness alone is generic, and so is an AI agent. What makes the Loom produce this institution's software — and what the live harness-map deliberately leaves out — is context. We treat it as a first-class asset: a regulated-entity brain the harnesses read from at every step and write back to after every cycle. It has a general-practice base that ships with the Loom, and on top of it the three dimensions that are the institution's own:

  • Regulated context — the regime's rules (what you must obey). The mandates and posture the entity is bound by: CBUAE / Al Tareq / Nebras obligations and deadlines, FAPI 2.0 security (mTLS, PAR, PKCE), BCBS 239 lineage, PDPL and UAE data residency, and the risk taxonomy & controls. In the repo this is a live seam — the data-risk register (controls.json, regulations.json, risk-taxonomy.json) that discovery’s D6 gate reads.
  • Solution domain — this problem space (what you're building). The shape of the thing itself: the Open Finance model, the OpenAPI contract (81 paths), the vendor-neutral ports P1–P9, the data model and retention tiers, the persona & scope matrix, and the synthetic dataset. Swap this dimension and the same Loom builds a different solution.
  • Institutional DNA — how the bank itself works. The entity’s own way of operating, the part no competitor shares: ways of working and approval routes (four-eyes, investment committee), architectural patterns and approved technologies, reusable code repositories & reference implementations, the design system & tokens, terminology, abbreviations & house style, and brand & tone of voice. In the repo this is the brand seam (discovery/brand/design.md) plus CLAUDE.md, the ADRs and working memory.

All three are seeded from what the institution already has, live in one governed place rather than in people’s heads, and compound: every cycle deposits more, so the brain gets broader and deeper — and the next solution is faster, safer and more clearly the entity’s own.

This is the moat. The Loom is generic by design and therefore copyable; the trained brain is not. It is where general best practice and the institution's own identity meet — turning "a way to build" into this regulated entity's way of building. And it widens the lead over time: every solution makes the brain broader and deeper, so each next build is cheaper and faster than it would be for anyone starting cold. A competitor can buy the same tools; they cannot buy years of the institution's accumulated, governed context. The brain only compounds in our favour if it is curated: bad context compounds as readily as good, so it is versioned, access-controlled and quality-checked, not merely accumulated.

Governed, not ad hoc. Because the brain is the institution's most valuable asset here, it is treated like one — access-controlled, versioned and auditable, governed like any regulated record. It has owners and a change history; it is not loose notes in people's heads.

Always-onContinuous assurance — agents, not meetings

Standing up the Loom is one thing; keeping a regulated entity compliant forever is another. Traditionally that means humans in the loop and an endless calendar of review, risk, control and sign-off meetings — and assurance that is only ever as fresh as the last one. Here the harness pulls specialised agents into that same lifecycle continuously — on every change, on a schedule, and on events — so every audit and regulatory commitment is continuously evidenced and pre-checked — and confirmed by accountable humans.

Each step that used to be a meeting is now run by an agent — watch the regulatory horizon, assess risk and impact, check spec & hard-stop conformance, test the controls and quality gates, capture the audit trail and BCBS 239 lineage, then confirm and report. The loop re-runs on the next trigger; it is never "done." Humans are not removed — they are reserved for genuine judgment and the four-eyes approvals that must stay human. What changes is that the recurring assurance becomes continuous and evidence-by-construction, not a periodic scramble — and every cycle writes back to the regulated-entity brain.

Agents, precisely. The harness now has four checked-in review agents (contract-conformance, hard-stop, data-governance, discovery-boundary) and an autonomous build loop (the next-story skill): one invocation builds one backlog item end to end and merges on green, story after story, with the human as an auditor of build-log.md rather than a mid-iteration gatekeeper. That loop has driven 134 of ~139 backlog items to done. A separate discovery skill runs the front diamond. There is also an experimental agent-first MCP gateway (services/mcp-gateway) that turns the BFF contract into agent tools without adding any enforcement of its own. The runtime continuous-assurance monitors, by contrast, are still deterministic scheduled jobs — making those always-on agents is the remaining roadmap.
Real vs nascent, stated plainly. Both harnesses are now substantial and checked in: delivery (gates, hooks, four reviewer agents, the autonomous loop, sealed evidence) and discovery (its own canon, D1–D8 gates, two skills, two reviewer agents and two real end-to-end runs). The HG-0001..0008 controls and an experimental agent-first MCP gateway round it out. What is still nascent is the runtime continuous-assurance layer — those monitors are deterministic scheduled jobs, not yet autonomous agents — and everything remains on synthetic data, pre-production.

Open Finance Back Office — the full example architecture

System context — who talks to what

Two ingresses reach the BFF Worker (admin-scoped): the operator portal (humans) and an agents interface — services/mcp-gateway (@ofbo/mcp-gateway, ADR 0017), an experimental agent-first surface that turns each @ofbo/contracts route into an MCP tool. It is a pure contract client: it adds no enforcement primitive of its own — auth, RBAC, four-eyes (202 + approval_request), INSERT-only audit and lineage all stay BFF-side (defence in depth). It is read-only by default; mutating tools stay gated on BACKOFFICE-53 spend-control plus a human raising a persona’s budget, and agent identities are issued via DCR (BACKOFFICE-60, ADR 0018). The BFF’s own agents module is the authoritative persona catalogue; the gateway is not in the deploy pipeline. The application core is DEPLOY_PROFILE-agnostic; shared packages and the simulated externals (Nebras, Supabase, Cloudflare) sit underneath, and all Nebras-bound traffic leaves only through P6 egress.

Ports (P1–P9) — every external system is an interface

packages/ports/src/interfaces.ts defines the contract; registry.ts is the only place DEPLOY_PROFILE is read. Core code calls getAdapter(port, profile) and never branches on profile itself. Two adapters per port behind one interface — sim (demo) and enterprise (M6 port-swap); contract tests bind both, which is the port-swap acceptance gate.

Worked example — fraud-suspected revoke (four-eyes)

BACKOFFICE-22: a Risk analyst posts a fraud revocation; the BFF requests a four-eyes approval and returns 202 + approval_request instead of acting inline. A second Risk approver approves, the BFF revokes through P6 egress with reason FRAUD_SUSPECTED, gets a sub-5s acknowledgement, writes a consent_revoked event to the High-class audit trail with an STR draft reference, and returns 200. PSU notification is deferred per fraud policy; STR submission is out of scope (BACKOFFICE-63).

Being honestLimits & what's next

What we can claim today, and what we can't — stated plainly, because a method worth adopting names its own risks.

  • Proven on a demo, not yet in production. The Open Finance Back Office runs on synthetic data and is permanently non-production. It proves the method produces a real, gated, audit-evidenced system; it has not yet cleared a live regulator examination or production scale.
  • "Any domain" is early evidence, not a measured result. The Loom was proven on one bounded, greenfield domain, and the discovery harness has now run a second problem (fee-variance reconciliation) through the same machinery — an encouraging signal that the method generalises. But legacy integration, real data and organisational change are the true costs elsewhere, and are not in the cost curve.
  • The cost / moat curve is illustrative — directional, not measured ROI.
  • The brain must be curated. Bad context compounds as readily as good, so it is governed, versioned and quality-checked, not merely accumulated.
  • AI model risk is real. The agents need ongoing validation, monitoring and independent challenge (see AI governance).
  • The build loop is autonomous; runtime assurance is not yet. An autonomous loop (the next-story skill) now builds story after story with the human as auditor — but the continuous-assurance monitors are still deterministic scheduled jobs, not autonomous agents.
  • People change is required. "Agents, not meetings" reshapes roles across engineering, risk and audit; adoption is a change programme, not just a tool.

Next: take one or two real domains through the Loom under full production governance, with internal audit and the regulator engaged early — turning "proven on a demo" into "proven in production."

Going forwardHow we build from here

Treat this as the template, not the exception. The discovery harness gives leadership a disciplined way to fund the right problems and stop the wrong ones early. The delivery harness gives teams a factory where control is automatic. The brain makes both improve with every use.

Because every external system is a port and every control is a gate, the same machine adapts to a new product, a new domain, a new regulator or a new vendor. Scaling means adding work to the line — not rebuilding it.
